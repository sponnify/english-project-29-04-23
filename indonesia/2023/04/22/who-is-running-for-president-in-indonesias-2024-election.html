Revealed: How Corruption is Destroying Jakarta's Society

Dear fellow citizens of Indonesia,

We are facing a crucial problem that threatens the very fabric of our society.

The corruption and bribery that have long plagued our government and police force have now reached a threshold that is damaging to our nation's well-being. As a developing country, we cannot afford to let this problem continue any longer.

The legacy of Dutch colonialism created an environment in which corruption was deeply ingrained in society. Under the authoritarian New Order regime in the second half of the 20th century, corruption became institutionalized, with government and police force systems infiltrated by corrupt practices, leading to further systemic deterioration. Scholars posit that the authoritarian legacy of President Suharto has contributed to a culture of corruption that has permeated Indonesian politics and society.

The implications of this corruption are vast and far-reaching. It undermines the very foundations of our democracy and cripples our ability to progress as a society. The government officials and police officers who engage in these illicit activities are acting out of self-interest and personal gain, rather than the well-being of our city and its people.

Research conducted by Josi Khatarina and her colleagues in 2013 revealed that nearly two thirds of Indonesians have experienced or have knowledge of some form of bribery or other illicit acts involving government officials. Transparency International reports that over 90% of citizens believe that there is a lack of control over corrupt activities in the country. These statistics show that corruption is not only widespread but also deeply ingrained within our society.

Recent protests in Papua against racial discrimination and police brutality show an extraordinary level of mistrust in the police force. The role of police leadership is fundamental; yet, officials continue to engage in corrupt practices, jeopardizing the government's ability to protect the country and serve its citizens.

However,

It is important to acknowledge that corruption is not a monolithic problem and that different regions and sectors may be impacted in different ways. Corruption in Indonesia's oil and gas industry, for example, is often facilitated by foreign corporations and states, complicating local anti-corruption efforts.

We must take action to address the issue of corruption in our country and demand accountability from those in power. It is not enough to simply hope that things will get better on their own. We must actively work to root out corruption and create a system that is transparent and accountable to its citizens.

To achieve this, we must come together as a community and demand change. We must pressure our elected officials to act with integrity and hold them accountable for their actions. We must also support independent watchdog organizations that are dedicated to rooting out corruption and promoting transparency.

It's time for us to take a stand against corruption and reclaim our city from those who seek to exploit it for personal gain. Together, we can build a brighter future for Jakarta and ensure that our city is a place where justice, fairness, and equality reign supreme.

Sincerely,

Denzel Edwards.
